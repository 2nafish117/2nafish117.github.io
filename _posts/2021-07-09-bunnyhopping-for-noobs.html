Bunnyhopping or strafe jumping is a general technique in (some) games that let you move faster than the base movement speed. Originally found in quake, because of an unintended bug (it's a feature damn it) in the game's movement code. Many other games like half life, counter strike, quake 2 and 3 and more recent games like valorant, apex legends, Doom 2016 and Eternal, titanfall still have it in one form or another. Some developers choose to embrace it while others try to get rid of it, either way it is a very interesting technique and is worth understanding how it works.

From my expirience playing those games and browsing the internet this is what i've learnt. Here is also a small demo that implements the quake style bunnyhopping. Try beating my top speed of 32.9 m/s.

The Bu ... I mean Feature

The piece of code responsible for this is the accelerate function that takes in arguments:
_velocity (the current velocity)
move_direction (intended direction of movement, also called wishdir in the original Quake source)
speed_max (the supposedly maximum speed achieveable)
accel_max (the maximum acceleration)
delta (the time delta between previous frame and current frame)

func accelerate(_velocity: Vector3, move_direction: Vector3, speed_max: float, accel_max: float, delta: float) -> Vector3:
	var projection = _velocity.dot(move_direction)
	var add_speed := clamp(speed_max - projection, 0.0, accel_max * delta)
	_velocity += add_speed * move_direction
	return _velocity

This part might be a hard to understand for those who dont know what vectors and vector dot product mean. I suggest reading on those before attempting to continue.

Let us try and understand this function. When you start moving forward from rest, the move_direction is along the same direction as _velocity, and the _velocity.dot(move_direction) is going to be the same as the magnitude of _velocity. When the magnitude of _velocity reaches speed_max, clamp(speed_max - projection, 0.0, accel_max * delta) returns 0.0.

clamp(num, min max) basically returns num if it is in the range min to max, returns min or max depending on it is smaller or larger than the range respectively.

So what happens when move_direction is not along _velocity?
This happens when you change the direction in which you move, and then the _velocity follows it. _velocity.dot(move_direction) could return a value less than max_speed even though the magintude of _velocity is equal to max_speed.

For example if max_speed is 8.0, magnitude of _velocity is 8.0, and the angle between move_direction and _velocity is 30 deg. the value of _velocity.dot(move_direction) is going to be 8.0 * cos(30 deg), which is 6.92. This means that it is possible to add speed to _velocity beyond max_speed!!

To consistently keep the velocity lagging behind the move direction, you need to constantly change the direction in which you move by moving your mouse left or right, along with the direction key you hold down. To keep this momentum to go from A to B you need to switch the direction you move your mouse along with your direction keys.
